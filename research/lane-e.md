# Lane E — UX & Accessibility

Research date: 2026-07-23. Audience: parents (wide age/tech-comfort range) buying school uniforms on mobile. Legal context: US private religious school. Sources labeled: [primary], [secondary], [vendor]. Staleness (>18 mo) and non-US findings flagged.

---

## 1. Checkout evidence (mobile-first commerce)

- **Average cart abandonment ~70.22%** — Baymard aggregate of ~50 studies, list maintained/updated into 2026. [primary] https://baymard.com/lists/cart-abandonment-rate
- **Top preventable abandonment reasons (Baymard survey, US shoppers):** extra costs (shipping/tax/fees) 48%; **forced account creation 26%**; payment-security concerns ~25%; slow delivery 23%; **too long/complicated checkout 22%**. [primary data via secondary roundup] https://www.shno.co/marketing-statistics/checkout-conversion-statistics (verify exact percentages against Baymard before publishing externally)
- **Form field bloat:** average US checkout shows **23.48 form elements** (14.88 actual fields) by default; Baymard says most checkouts can cut **20–60%** of elements — only ~6–8 fields are truly necessary for most orders. [primary via secondary] https://baymard.com/lists/cart-abandonment-rate ; https://growth-engines.com/insights/ecommerce/ecommerce-checkout-optimization
- **Upside:** average large e-commerce site can gain **~35.26% conversion uplift** from checkout UX fixes alone (Baymard benchmark). [primary] https://baymard.com/lists/cart-abandonment-rate
- **Guest checkout:** offering guest checkout lowers abandonment ~14% vs. mandatory registration (Baymard-derived figure). [secondary] https://www.ringly.io/blog/ecommerce-cart-abandonment-statistics-2026 — Implication: never force account creation to buy a uniform; offer "create account after purchase."
- **Express payment buttons (Apple Pay / Google Pay / PayPal):** Baymard's Payment UX research: show wallets as distinct, clearly labeled buttons **above the card form**, but let users opt in — don't aggressively funnel. Wallets reduce form-filling to near zero for enrolled users. [primary] https://baymard.com/learn/payment-ux — Claimed **10–15% mobile conversion uplift** from adding Apple Pay is a [vendor] figure: https://ecomhint.com/blog/mobile-checkout-best-practices
- **Address autocomplete:** speeds completion and cuts typos, biggest win on mobile where typing is slow/error-prone; claimed **30–70% reduction in delivery failures** is a [vendor] figure. https://www.checkoutwc.com/blog/checkout-ux-causes-failed-orders/ ; general practice endorsed in Baymard checkout research. [primary/vendor mix]
- **Mobile speed:** Google: **53% of mobile visits abandoned if load >3s**. [primary but STALE — 2016/2017 study; direction still accepted] https://www.marketingdive.com/news/google-53-of-mobile-users-abandon-sites-that-take-over-3-seconds-to-load/426070/ ; https://blog.google/products/admanager/the-need-for-mobile-speed/

## 2. Account / login friction

- **Password resets kill checkouts:** Baymard testing found **up to ~19% checkout abandonment among existing-account users solely due to password/reset issues** (reset emails slow, users give up). [primary but STALE — Baymard blog originally ~2016–2017; mechanism still valid] https://baymard.com/blog/password-requirements-and-password-reset
- **Password resets as support burden:** password issues = **20–50% of help-desk tickets** (Gartner, relayed by vendors); ~**$70 per reset ticket** (HDI/Forrester, relayed by vendors). [vendor-relayed; treat as order-of-magnitude] https://www.hypr.com/blog/how-much-does-a-password-reset-cost ; https://www.avatier.com/blog/password-resets-for-help-desk-tickets/ — For a small school store, every reset is a call to the front office; design to avoid passwords entirely.
- **Magic links (passwordless email login):** single-field login; works well for wide tech-comfort audiences ("everyone can click an email link"); eliminates credential stuffing. Known pitfalls: depends on email access; user may switch apps mid-flow; **iOS opens links in the default browser, breaking same-browser flows** — mitigate with resend option, clear expiry messaging, and OTP-code fallback. [vendor docs, but authoritative on mechanics] https://auth0.com/docs/authenticate/passwordless/authentication-methods/email-magic-link ; https://www.scalekit.com/blog/otp-vs-magic-links-passwordless-authentication
- **Family accounts:** parent-portal norm = **one parent login, multiple linked child profiles**, switch between children in one dashboard, and **pay for multiple siblings in one transaction**. Guardian-level permissions (custodial vs. non-custodial) exist in mature systems. [vendor docs; School Bytes/Arbor are NON-US (AU/UK) but pattern is universal] https://parentportalhelp.com/faq/ ; https://support.schoolbytes.education/hc/en-us/articles/5184859634191-Parent-Portal-Set-up-your-parent-portal-account — For a uniform store: child profiles should carry name/grade/sizes so reorders are one tap.

## 3. Accessibility law (US, private religious school)

- **ADA Title III religious exemption:** "religious organizations or entities controlled by religious organizations, including places of worship" are **exempt from Title III entirely** (42 U.S.C. §12187). Exemption is broad — covers ALL facilities/programs/activities, secular or religious, even when open to the public. A school controlled by a religious entity keeps the exemption. [primary] https://archive.ada.gov/taman3.htm (DOJ Title III TA Manual III-1.5000) ; https://adata.org/factsheet/religious-entities/
  - **Caveat:** exemption must be *proven* — courts have let ADA suits proceed where religious control of the school was in doubt (e.g., independent board, loose denominational ties). [secondary, court coverage] https://www.churchlawandtax.com/legal-developments/court-ada-case-brought-against-religious-school-allowed-to-proceed/
- **Section 508:** applies **only to federal agencies** (and federal procurement) — not to private schools. [primary] https://www.section508.gov/blog/do-section-508-accessibility-standards-apply-to-mywebsite/
  - **But Section 504** (Rehabilitation Act) applies to **any recipient of federal financial assistance** — incl. private schools taking federal funds (lunch programs, E-Rate, some grants). Check the school's funding before relying on the Title III exemption. [secondary] https://www.echalk.com/legal-requirements
- **DOJ web rule (2024) is Title II only:** requires **WCAG 2.1 AA** for state/local government web content and apps — **does NOT cover private schools**. Compliance dates extended by an April 2026 interim final rule: large entities Apr 26 2027, small entities/special districts Apr 26 2028. [primary] https://www.ada.gov/resources/2024-03-08-web-rule/ ; https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/04/20/2026-07663/extension-of-compliance-dates-for-nondiscrimination-on-the-basis-of-disability-accessibility-of-web
- **State law — California Unruh Act:** statutory damages **min. $4,000/occurrence**; ~787 CA web-accessibility suits in 2025 (+62% YoY). *Martinez v. Cot'n Wash* (Cal. Ct. App. 2022): online-only businesses without a physical-place nexus fall outside the Unruh/ADA-incorporation theory (intentional-discrimination claims still possible). Relevance depends on whether the school serves CA residents; NY and FL are the other high-volume venues. [secondary/court coverage] https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en-us/knowledge/publications/08b28fd0/ca-court-shuts-down-website-accessibility-claims-for-online-only-businesses ; https://legalclarity.org/ada-lawsuits-in-california-unruh-act-defenses-and-reforms/
- **Litigation climate 2025:** ~5,114 digital-accessibility suits filed (federal + state); **e-commerce = ~70% of targets**; suits against sites *using accessibility overlay widgets* keep rising (1,416 widget-equipped companies sued in 2025; ~22.6% of H1-2025 filings) — **overlays are not a defense**. [primary-ish industry tracker (UsableNet, a vendor, but the standard citation)] https://info.usablenet.com/hubfs/2025-MidYear-Report-FINAL.pdf ; https://blog.usablenet.com/ada-web-lawsuit-trends-2026
- **De facto standard:** WCAG 2.1 AA is what DOJ codified (Title II) and what settlements/consent decrees reference; **WCAG 2.2** (W3C Recommendation, Oct 5 2023) is the current version — target 2.2 AA to be future-proof. [primary] https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/ ; https://www.ada.gov/resources/2024-03-08-web-rule/
- **Bottom line:** the school likely has a strong Title III exemption argument (if genuinely religiously controlled), but Section 504 (if federal funds), state laws, demand letters, and mission/reputation all point the same way — build to WCAG 2.2 AA anyway; it is cheap at design time and the exemption only pays off *after* litigation.

## 4. Practical accessibility for this audience (parents, mixed age/tech comfort)

- **Font size:** minimum **16px body text**; HHS Health Literacy Online recommends **larger (~19px+) for older adults / low vision**; use relative units (rem/em) and respect OS text scaling (iOS Dynamic Type / Android font settings). [primary, gov] https://odphp.health.gov/healthliteracyonline/design-easy-scanning/use-readable-font-thats-least-16-pixels ; https://www.section508.gov/develop/fonts-typography/
- **Tap targets:** WCAG 2.2 AA new SC 2.5.8 = **min 24×24 CSS px** (or equivalent spacing); Apple/Google platform guidance remains 44pt/48dp — use the larger for primary actions (size selectors, quantity steppers, "Add to cart"). [primary] https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/#target-size-minimum
- **Plain language:** write at ~6th–8th-grade level, front-load key info, short sentences, no jargon ("PE kit (gym clothes)"), per federal plain-language guidance. [primary, gov] https://www.plainlanguage.gov/guidelines/ ; https://odphp.health.gov/healthliteracyonline/
- **Low bandwidth / performance:** parents shop from carpool lines on cellular — keep pages light (compress images, lazy-load, minimal JS), target LCP <2.5s (Core Web Vitals); 53%-abandon-at-3s stat above [STALE but directionally sound]. https://web.dev/articles/vitals
- **Audience-specific patterns:** guest checkout + optional magic-link account; saved child sizes for reorder; one order for multiple kids; visible order confirmation via email (parents forward to spouses); avoid overlay widgets (legal + UX liability, §3); test with real parents including grandparents/guardians.

---

## Top 5 takeaways

1. **Kill checkout friction first:** guest checkout, ≤8 form fields, address autocomplete, and Apple Pay/Google Pay buttons above the card form — Baymard's data ties 26% of abandonment to forced accounts and 22% to long checkouts, with ~35% conversion upside from UX fixes alone.
2. **Go passwordless:** magic-link login (with OTP fallback) removes the single biggest self-inflicted wound — password resets, which cause up to ~19% checkout abandonment for account holders and dominate small-org support load.
3. **Model the family, not the shopper:** one parent account, multiple child profiles with saved sizes/grade, one payment for all siblings — the established parent-portal pattern.
4. **Legal posture:** ADA Title III likely doesn't apply (religious-entity exemption, if the school is genuinely religiously controlled) and Section 508 and the DOJ Title II rule definitely don't — but Section 504 (federal funds), state laws like California's Unruh Act ($4k/occurrence), and a litigation climate where ~70% of suits hit e-commerce mean WCAG 2.2 AA is still the right build target. Do not rely on overlay widgets — sued sites increasingly have them installed.
5. **Design for the least-technical parent on a phone on cellular:** 16px+ text (19px for older users), 24px minimum / 44px preferred tap targets, ~6th–8th-grade plain language, and pages that load in under ~3 seconds on a weak connection.
