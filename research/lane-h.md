# Lane H — Edge Cases

Adversarial / cross-cutting edge-case hunt for the parent-facing uniform & supplies portal (small US private Christian school, pickup + shipping, back-to-school spike, limited staff, July 2026). Scenarios other lanes likely miss. Claims with sources have URLs; everything else is labeled **REASONED (no source)**. Stale (>18 mo) and non-US findings flagged.

---

## 1. Family structure

- **Divorced/separated parents both create accounts for the same student** — duplicate accounts split order history, break "one uniform allotment per student" logic, and produce double purchases neither parent knows about. Custody/school entanglement is documented as a growing problem for private schools ([Fisher Phillips](https://www.fisherphillips.com/en/news-insights/private-and-independent-schools-custody-disputes.html)). Why it matters: staff get dragged into refund fights they can't adjudicate.
- **Custody dispute over pickup** — Parent A orders, Parent B (or B's new partner) shows up at the front office during their custody day to collect. Custody orders on file govern who may remove a child, and schools are legally responsible for honoring them ([Schools Legal Service memo](https://schoolslegalservice.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/Memo-9-Custody-August-2022-ABH.pdf)). Why it matters: a T-shirt handoff can become a legal incident; the portal needs a "who may pick up" concept or an explicit policy that anyone with the order number may collect.
- **Portal used as a custody weapon** — one parent changes the shared contact email/address so the other stops receiving receipts and pickup notices; parents also try to use school staff as messengers/sides-takers ([Morales Law](https://www.mysantabarbaralawyer.com/blog/2026/march/handling-divorce-when-kids-are-in-school/)). Why it matters: notification design (per-account, not per-student) determines whether the school gets blamed.
- **Non-custodial parent demands order/spending records** — FERPA gives custodial and non-custodial parents equal rights absent a court order to the contrary ([ED FAQ](https://studentprivacy.ed.gov/faq/case-divorce-do-both-parents-have-rights-under-ferpa)). Why it matters: if store orders are tied to the student record, refusing one parent access may itself be a compliance/PR problem.
- **Grandparents/guardians/nannies buying** — REASONED (no source): they lack portal accounts, don't know sizes or homeroom, and may pay with a card whose name matches no roster entry. Why it matters: guest checkout vs. roster-gated access is a real design fork; blocking them shifts work to office staff.
- **Staff/teachers buying for their own kids (or with staff discount)** — REASONED (no source): staff may have admin access to the same tool they shop in; discount eligibility and self-service refunds create a small-scale conflict-of-interest/audit gap. Why it matters: tiny schools rarely separate roles; one shared login can void financial controls.
- **Blended families / different last names** — REASONED (no source): payer surname ≠ student surname is the norm, not the exception; any "match card name to family" fraud heuristic will false-positive. Why it matters: legitimate orders get flagged or lost at pickup sorting.
- **Two households, one shipment** — REASONED (no source): items ship to the house where the student isn't that week; "ship to address on file" defaults are wrong for ~every shared-custody family. Why it matters: reshipping costs and blame land on the school.

## 2. Identity / access

- **Semi-private store discoverability** — REASONED (no source): a link-only or shared-password storefront leaks via forwarded emails and Facebook groups; outsiders can buy logo gear (uniform items double as school-affiliation identifiers — a child-safety optics issue for a small Christian school). Why it matters: "security by URL" fails silently; decide up front whether outsider purchases are actually harmful or fine.
- **Withdrawn/alumni families retain access** — REASONED (no source): without roster sync, a family that left in May can still order in August (and expect financial-aid pricing, pickup rights, or FACTS billing that no longer exists). Why it matters: refunds, awkward conversations, and stale-credit disputes at the worst staffing moment.
- **Mid-year enrollments** — REASONED (no source): new families need uniforms *before* they appear in the SIS/roster export; the access gate blocks exactly the people with the most urgent need. Why it matters: every gate needs a manual-add path the front office can execute in minutes.
- **Roster sync drift** — REASONED (no source): re-enrollment season (spring) and August onboarding mean the roster is most wrong exactly when store traffic peaks; siblings, re-admits, and name changes (marriage, adoption) break matching. Why it matters: "computer says you're not a family here" during back-to-school is the fastest way to lose parent trust.
- **Parent account takeover** — credential-stuffed parent accounts are plausible given mass reuse; the PowerSchool breach (Dec 2024, largest known breach of US children's data; attacker used one compromised support credential) shows K-12 ecosystems are actively targeted ([TechTarget](https://www.techtarget.com/whatis/feature/PowerSchool-data-breach-Explaining-how-it-happened), [NBC](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/security/powerschool-hack-data-breach-protect-student-school-teacher-safe-rcna189029)). Why it matters: a taken-over account exposes home address, kids' names/sizes/school — a stalking-grade bundle; saved cards make it monetizable.
- **Shared family email = shared account** — REASONED (no source): many households use one email for school; password resets and 2FA then hinge on whoever controls that inbox (see custody weaponization above). Why it matters: account-recovery flow is a custody and security surface, not a convenience feature.

## 3. Money edges

- **Financial-aid families and discreet subsidy** — NAIS research: uniforms/extras add 10–20%+ beyond tuition; aid families report stigma, and confidentiality plus *proactive automatic* application of support is what prevents exclusion ([NAIS executive summary](https://www.nais.org/resource-center/resources/research/executive-summary-of-prioritizing-belonging-for-pk-12-families-on-financial-assistance-in-private-s)). Why it matters: a voucher code a parent must type at checkout (or a visibly discounted price at pickup) outs them; subsidy must be invisible in UI, receipts, and pickup lists.
- **Scholarship/uniform-fund purchases** — REASONED (no source): church or PTO uniform funds want to pay for specific families without knowing card details or being named on receipts; who "owns" the order for returns? Why it matters: third-party-payer orders break the payer=parent assumption baked into refunds and communication.
- **FACTS incidental billing instead of card** — FACTS incidental billing lets schools push store charges onto the family's tuition account; many schools require all families to keep an incidental account ([FACTS](https://factsmgt.com/features/accounting-billing-payments/), [example school flyer](https://sacredheartva.org/wp-content/uploads/FACTS-Incidental-Billing-Flyer-1.pdf)). Why it matters: parents will ask "just bill my FACTS account"; if the store can't, staff will do it manually (reconciliation drift); if it can, a store order can now trigger tuition-delinquency consequences — and delinquent families may use the store to run up a balance.
- **Price changes between order and pickup** — REASONED (no source): vendor cost increases mid-summer; orders placed in June picked up in September at the old price, or (worse) staff re-ring items at pickup at the new price. Why it matters: honor-the-order-price must be an explicit rule or every price update creates disputes.
- **Partial payments / layaway requests** — REASONED (no source): a full uniform set for 3 kids can be several hundred dollars in one August transaction; families will ask to pay half now. Why it matters: platforms mostly don't support it; the workaround (staff-held IOUs) is untracked debt.
- **Card testing / carding attacks on the checkout** — small/nonprofit checkouts are prime card-testing targets; bots run thousands of small transactions; Stripe blocked 20M+ attempts/day at the 2022 peak (**stale, >18 mo** but pattern persists) ([Stripe](https://stripe.com/newsroom/news/card-testing-surge), [Give Lively](https://www.givelively.org/resources/protect-against-credit-card-testing)); one nonprofit saw 1,727 fraudulent attempts before its processor suspended the account ([Digital Defense Fund](https://digitaldefensefund.org/learn/protecting-your-organization-from-a-fraudulent-payment-attack)). Why it matters: fees + chargebacks + a *processor account suspension during back-to-school week* would take the whole store down.
- **Chargeback as dispute-resolution** — REASONED (no source): a parent unhappy with sizing/refund policy disputes the card charge instead of talking to school; small merchants nearly always lose or eat fees, and the "merchant" is the school the family attends. Why it matters: chargeback policy, evidence capture (pickup signature), and relationship management are intertwined.
- **Sales tax on uniform sales** — treatment varies sharply by state (e.g., Utah exempts school-sold required uniforms; Maryland says schools should collect tax on gym uniforms/supplies students keep; Texas gives limited tax-free sale days) ([Utah Pub 35](https://tax.utah.gov/forms-pubs/pub-35/), [MCPS FAQ](https://www.montgomeryschoolsmd.org/departments/sharedaccountability/salestax), [Texas Comptroller](https://comptroller.texas.gov/taxes/publications/94-183.php)). Why it matters: "we're a nonprofit so no tax" is often wrong for *sales*; shipping to another state adds nexus questions. Verify for the school's state before launch.
- **Refund to a card that no longer exists** — REASONED (no source): August order, October refund, card since reissued/canceled (or paid by ex-spouse's card, or by grandparent). Why it matters: store-credit vs. original-payment refund policy must handle payer≠parent and dead cards.

## 4. Order lifecycle edges

- **Orders never picked up** — REASONED (no source): paid orders sit at the front office for months; family forgets, or student withdrew. Why it matters: needs an aging policy (remind → convert to shipment → refund/donate) or the office becomes a warehouse of ambiguous school-owned/family-owned goods.
- **Student leaves before pickup/delivery** — REASONED (no source): family withdraws (sometimes on bad terms, sometimes owing tuition) with a paid, unfetched order; school may want to offset refund against balance owed. Why it matters: refund-vs-offset is a policy landmine; decide before it happens.
- **Wrong-student / wrong-family pickup** — REASONED (no source): same last names, sibling pickups, teacher grabs a bag for a carpool; verification at pickup is a smile and a first name. Why it matters: one mis-released order = lost goods + a parent who "paid and got nothing"; combine with custody scenarios above and it's worse.
- **Lost orders at the front office** — REASONED (no source): no chain of custody between "fulfilled online" and "in a bin behind the desk"; volunteer sorters during the spike. Why it matters: the digital system says delivered, physical reality disagrees; a pickup-scan/signature step is the only reconciliation.
- **Duplicate orders from double-tap/refresh** — REASONED (no source): slow checkout during the August spike → parent taps Pay twice or refreshes → duplicate charge and duplicate fulfillment, decrementing scarce inventory twice. Why it matters: well-known pattern (idempotency keys exist for a reason) but deadly when inventory is 1 unit deep per size.
- **Send-to-classroom fulfillment** — REASONED (no source): "we'll send it home in the student's backpack" is the small-school default and the least auditable channel (wrong kid, absent kid, item never arrives home). Why it matters: cheapest fulfillment mode has the highest dispute rate; if offered, it needs a handed-to-student log.
- **Shipping to PO boxes / rural addresses / address typos** — REASONED (no source): one-person ops means no address validation review; carrier returns land back at school mid-spike. Why it matters: each failed shipment costs double postage plus a support thread.
- **Order placed for the wrong child** — REASONED (no source): multi-student families pick the wrong kid in a dropdown; embroidered/personalized items become unreturnable. Why it matters: personalization + child-selector UX errors = pure loss; confirm student on the review screen.

## 5. Data / privacy

- **COPPA — verified: obligations attach to operators collecting personal info *from children under 13*, not from parents** ([FTC COPPA FAQ](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/complying-coppa-frequently-asked-questions), [16 CFR 312](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-C/part-312)). A parents-only store is out of COPPA scope *unless* students themselves can log in/browse/wishlist — and the FTC finalized a strengthened rule in Jan 2025 ([FTC](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/01/ftc-finalizes-changes-childrens-privacy-rule-limiting-companies-ability-monetize-kids-data)). Why it matters: one "let students pick their own spirit wear" feature flips the compliance posture; keep accounts adult-only.
- **FERPA and rosters in a commerce tool** — pushing the student roster into a third-party store platform is a disclosure of education records; it only fits FERPA via directory information (with proper notice/opt-out) or the school-official exception (direct control, no re-disclosure, authorized use only) ([ED vendor FAQ](https://studentprivacy.ed.gov/sites/default/files/resource_document/file/Vendor%20FAQ.pdf); PTAC guidance — **stale, 2014**: [PTAC](https://studentprivacy.ed.gov/sites/default/files/resource_document/file/Student%20Privacy%20and%20Online%20Educational%20Services%20(February%202014)_0.pdf)). Note: FERPA applies to schools receiving federal funds — many private schools aren't covered, but should confirm rather than assume. Why it matters: the platform's ToS may claim marketing rights over uploaded customer data; minimize what syncs (name + grade beats full record).
- **Vendor breach exposes children's data at scale** — PowerSchool (Dec 2024): one compromised credential → data on tens of millions of students; extortionists later re-targeted individual districts ([TechTarget](https://www.techtarget.com/whatis/feature/PowerSchool-data-breach-Explaining-how-it-happened), [EdWeek](https://www.edweek.org/technology/what-schools-should-know-about-the-powerschool-data-breach/2025/01)). Why it matters: whatever platform is chosen inherits this threat model; store the minimum (no birthdates, no student IDs, no addresses beyond shipping).
- **Email deliverability** — school/mass-sender email routinely lands in spam; districts publish troubleshooting guides for exactly this; ~1 in 6 emails never reaches the inbox ([EAB](https://eab.com/resources/blog/enrollment-blog/why-your-messages-are-flagged-as-spam/), [district example](https://www.sowashco.org/about-us/news/article/~board/news/post/spam-school-messages)). Why it matters: order confirmations, pickup notices, and password resets silently failing = phantom "you never told me" disputes; set up SPF/DKIM/DMARC on the sending domain and offer SMS for pickup notices.
- **Store impersonation / fake fee phishing** — scammers impersonate schools with fake fee-payment requests; UK private-school parents lose £3,200 on average per successful scam (**non-US**, Jan 2026: [IRIS research](https://tools.prnewswire.com/en-us/live/20813/release/20260119EN65898)); US independent-school association warns of sophisticated tuition phishing ([NBOA](https://www.nboa.org/net-assets/article/sophisticated-new-tuition-phishing-scams--what-schools-need-to-know)). Why it matters: launching a new "pay the school online here" habit trains parents to click payment links; announce one canonical URL, never send pay-links in email.
- **Pickup lists as a privacy leak** — REASONED (no source): a printed alphabetized pickup sheet at the front desk exposes every family's purchases (and, if subsidies are visible, aid status) to any parent volunteer. Why it matters: the offline artifacts of the online store leak more than the store does.
- **Photos/size data of children** — REASONED (no source): storing per-child measurements/sizes is convenient but is a child-linked profile in a commerce database. Why it matters: keep sizes on the order, not on a persistent child profile, unless there's a real reuse case.

## 6. Calendar edges

- **Summer shutdown vs. July–August ordering peak** — REASONED (no source): office is closed/skeleton-staffed exactly when order volume, new-family onboarding, and roster churn peak. Why it matters: support SLAs, pickup windows, and manual account-adds all assume staff who aren't there; pre-schedule volunteer pickup days.
- **New-family onboarding in August** — REASONED (no source): families receive portal invite + tuition setup + supply lists + store link in one overwhelming week; store emails compete with (and resemble) the phishing pattern above. Why it matters: sequence communications; one canonical checklist page beats five emails.
- **Weather/emergency closure during pickup week** — REASONED (no source): hurricane/ice day cancels announced pickup days; hundreds of orders re-scheduled with no notification channel but the (spam-prone) email. Why it matters: pickup scheduling needs a reschedule-broadcast path (SMS) and slack in the calendar.
- **Mid-year uniform policy change** — parents protest uniform rule changes loudly and publicly (**non-US example**: [BBC](https://feeds.bbci.co.uk/news/articles/crgg1zjvqzjo)). REASONED for the store angle: a policy change strands purchased-but-now-noncompliant inventory in families' closets and school stock. Why it matters: policy changes need a store plan (grandfathering, exchange window, sell-through) or the store becomes the lightning rod.
- **Uniform supply shortages near term start** — uniform stock runs short close to the start of term; shortages have forced schools to relax dress code temporarily (**non-US, stale**: [Mumsnet thread](https://www.mumsnet.com/talk/style_and_beauty/5376874-is-it-me-or-are-school-uniform-items-in-short-supply), [Gulf News](https://gulfnews.com/amp/story/uae%2Feducation%2Fshortage-students-attend-class-without-uniforms-1.1383286)). Why it matters: the store needs a public "out of stock ≠ dress-code violation" grace policy so the store's inventory failures don't become discipline incidents.
- **Fiscal/school-year boundary** — REASONED (no source): orders placed in July for next year hit this year's books; aid budgets and FACTS accounts roll over mid-summer. Why it matters: accounting cutoffs vs. commerce timestamps confuse tiny finance teams; tag orders with school year, not just date.

## Top 10 edge cases by likelihood × impact

1. **Email deliverability failure** (confirmations/pickup notices in spam) — near-certain at small scale; drives most "where's my order" load. (Sourced)
2. **Roster/access gate blocks new and mid-year families in August** — high likelihood; hits the most sympathetic users at peak. (REASONED)
3. **Orders never picked up / lost at the front office** — high likelihood with volunteer fulfillment; erodes trust and eats staff time. (REASONED)
4. **Duplicate orders from double-tap during the spike** — high likelihood on slow checkout; double-charges + phantom inventory depletion. (REASONED)
5. **Divorced-parent duplicate accounts, notifications, and pickup conflicts** — moderate likelihood, outsized emotional/legal impact. (Sourced context)
6. **Financial-aid subsidy visible in checkout or pickup artifacts** — moderate likelihood, severe belonging/stigma damage at a mission-driven school. (Sourced context)
7. **Card testing attack or chargeback wave suspends the processor account in back-to-school week** — lower likelihood, catastrophic timing impact. (Sourced)
8. **FACTS-billing workaround debt** ("just put it on my account") — high likelihood at a FACTS school; silent reconciliation drift and tuition-delinquency entanglement. (Sourced context)
9. **Fake "pay your uniform fees" phishing riding the new store launch** — moderate likelihood, high dollar + trust impact. (Sourced)
10. **Roster sync to the commerce platform creating a FERPA/privacy exposure or breach blast radius** — lower likelihood, high severity; minimized by syncing minimum data. (Sourced)
